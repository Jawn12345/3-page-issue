About Sea Turtles

Sea turtles are an ancient species, dating back to about 220 million years ago. These ancient animals have lived in our waters since the time of the dinosaurs. Their average lifespan is about 30 to 100 years, but the upper limits are still unknowned to scientists. There are 7 different species of sea turtles living in the world today, and they are the Leatherback, Green, Hawksbill, Loggerhead, Olive Ridly, Kemp's Ridley, and Flatback.

Sea turtles are characterized by their shell and non-retractile head and limbs, meaning that they cannot put their heads and limbs inside their shell. The top part of their shell is called the carapace while the bottom part is called a plastrong. Most species of sea turtles, except the green turtle, are omnivorous. Some species of sea turtles specializes on a certain prey. For example, Hawksbill Sea Turtles primarily eat sea sponges and Leatherback Sea Turtles eat jellyfishes and sea squirts.

Sea turtles live most of their lives in the sea; however, they periodic come ashore to lay their eggs. Sea turtles have the ability of magnetoreception, or the ability to detect the Earth's magnetic field. They use this ability to help them return to their nesting grounds to lay their eggs. Like many species of reptiles, the temperature of the developing eggs is what ultimately determines the sex of the offspring. Higher temperatures produce more female offsprings while lower temperatures produce more male offsprings. After eggs hatch, baby sea turtles have to make a mad dash to ocean at night, avoiding predators such as birds and crabs. They have the tendency to move in the brightest direction, which is typically the moonlight reflected over the ocean.

Why are happening to Sea Turtles

Out of the 7 species of sea turtles, nearly all of them are classified as endangered or vulnerable species, with 2 of them being critically endangered. The 2 endangered species are the Hawksbill and the Kemp's Ridley. There are numerous reasons for the significant decline in sea turtle population. These include bycatch, light pollution, poaching, habitat destruction, platic waste, and more.
